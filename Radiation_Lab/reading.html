[1] Unlike conduction and convection, which require matter (atoms or molecules) to transfer energy, radiation is the transfer of energy through space by electromagnetic waves. This means heat can travel through a total vacuum—which is exactly how the Sun's energy reaches Earth across millions of miles of empty space.

The Microscopic View

[2] Everything in the universe that has a temperature above absolute zero emits radiation. On a microscopic level, this happens because the charged particles within atoms are constantly jiggling and vibrating. When these particles move, they create electromagnetic waves that carry energy away from the object. The hotter an object is, the faster its particles jiggle, and the more radiation it emits.

"Radiation is the only mode of heat transfer that does not require a medium."

Absorption and Reflection (Albedo)

[3] When radiation hits an object, it can be absorbed, reflected, or transmitted. The ability of a surface to reflect radiation is called its albedo. Surfaces with high albedo, like snow or a polished mirror, reflect most of the energy that hits them and stay relatively cool. Surfaces with low albedo, like dark asphalt or a black t-shirt, absorb most of the energy, causing their particles to vibrate faster and their temperature to rise.

The Inverse Square Law

[4] You might have noticed that a campfire feels much hotter when you stand right next to it than when you are ten feet away. This is because radiation spreads out as it travels. Since the energy is spreading over a larger area, its intensity decreases rapidly as the distance increases. Specifically, if you double your distance from a heat source, you only receive one-fourth of the energy. This is known as the Inverse Square Law.
