# Student-Fair Connections Puzzle Guidelines

Follow these rules whenever designing, updating, or generating a NYT Connections-style word grouping game for classroom lessons. The goal is to reinforce physics and science concepts rather than create confusion or trigger excessive trial-and-error.

## 1. Minimize Red Herrings (Overlap)
* **The Guideline**: Limit cross-category word overlap to **no more than 1 or 2 words total** per 16-word board.
* **Why**: High school students should be rewarded for conceptual sorting, not punished by complex elimination tricks that require guessing multiple combinations of the same definitions.

## 2. Standard Vocabulary Constraint
* **The Guideline**: All terms used must be explicitly taught in the current unit, defined in the homework, or be standard everyday English. 
* **Why**: The puzzle should test conceptual association and retrieval, not dictionary vocabulary.

## 3. Strict 1-2 Word Card Limits
* **The Guideline**: Card items must be **1 or 2 words maximum** (e.g., *Resistor*, *Kinetic Energy*). 
* **Why**: Short terms fit beautifully on mobile and desktop grids without breaking layouts or increasing visual cognitive load.

## 4. Balanced Difficulty Tiers
Structure the four categories with clear progressions:
* 🟨 **Yellow (Straightforward)**: Direct science associations or classifications (e.g. *Contact Forces*, *EM Waves*).
* 🟩 **Green (Conceptual)**: Core terms grouped by a shared characteristic or behavior (e.g. *Vector Quantities*, *Units of Energy*).
* 🟦 **Blue (Analytical)**: Equation or relational groups (e.g. *Variables in Ohm's Law*, *Terms that double when mass doubles*).
* 🟪 **Purple (Tricky)**: Light wordplay, fill-in-the-blank, or history (e.g. *Words preceding "Law" in physics*, *Units named after scientists*). Keep the wordplay simple and accessible!

## 5. No Layered Wordplay
* **The Guideline**: Avoid combining two layers of abstract logic (e.g., do not use homophones of physics terms, or words that anagram into units).
